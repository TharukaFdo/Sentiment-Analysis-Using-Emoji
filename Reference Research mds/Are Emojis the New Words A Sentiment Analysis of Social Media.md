ABSTRACT

Emojis have become an increasingly important aspect of consumer-brand
interactions in the Indian subcontinent. However, the impact of emoji use on brand
image and mental health remains underexplored, particularly in emerging
economies like India, where structured research on this topic is limited. To address
this gap, the present study analyzes over 4,600 consumer tweets related to 19
prominent brands across eleven industries. Using VADER sentiment analysis, the
research develops a metric to assess consumer sentiment and brand engagement in
relation to emoji usage. The findings indicate that effective integration of emojis
contributes to positive consumer sentiment and enhanced brand engagement. Given
that emojis have become a fundamental part of everyday communication, this study
recommends that brand and marketing managers closely monitor consumer
interactions with emojis, utilizing rich data to refine communication strategies and
maximize emoji-driven engagement.

INTRODUCTION

Over time, emojis have evolved from simple pictographs into a crucial component
of modern communication, marking a significant shift in the way individuals and
brands interact within the digital and social media landscape. Their use is
particularly prevalent in India’s intricate and diverse market, where emojis have
become instrumental in navigating the country’s rich linguistic and cultural
diversity. In India’s diverse and multilingual market, emojis serve as a universal
communication tool that helps brands engage effectively with consumers across
different linguistic and cultural backgrounds.

This study examines how emojis influence brand equity and consumer engagement
in India by analyzing sentiment in social media interactions. Using VADER
sentiment analysis on tweets related to prominent Indian brands, we explore
whether emojis function as mere decorative elements or strategic marketing tools.

In the dynamic and competitive Indian market, where brands vie for attention
across diverse cultural and linguistic communities, the use of emojis offers a way
to transcend the limitations of language. India’s linguistic diversity, with over 22
official languages and numerous dialects, often makes traditional text-based
communication a challenging endeavor for marketers. Emojis, however, function
as a common visual language that can be easily understood by a wide audience,
regardless of their linguistic background. This makes them particularly valuable in
a country like India, where a brand’s ability to connect with consumers across
regional and linguistic divides is critical to its success. For instance, emojis can
convey emotions, moods, and ideas in a way that text alone often cannot, enabling
brands to communicate more effectively with their target audiences.

Gen Z, the first digitally native generation, prefers fast and visually expressive
communication. Emojis align with their engagement style, making them a crucial
tool for brands aiming to connect with younger audiences on social media.

In India, where digital engagement is rapidly increasing among younger
consumers, the strategic use of emojis has become a powerful marketing tool.
Brands such as Swiggy, Zomato, and Flipkart have already recognized the potential
of emojis to create more engaging and relatable content, often using food and
shopping-related emojis to evoke emotions such as hunger, excitement, or
satisfaction. These brands have effectively utilized emojis to create personalized
and fun interactions with consumers, which not only drives engagement but also

# LITERATURE REVIEW

Emojis have become an essential part of digital communication, enriching
emotional expression and social interaction in text-based platforms where
traditional non-verbal cues are absent. Early research by Derks et al. (2007)
established that emoticons, the predecessors of modern emojis, clarify emotional
intent and strengthen interpersonal connections in socially rich online
environments. Expanding on this foundation, Kaye and Schweiger (2023)
investigated whether emojis serve as reliable indicators of users’ immediate
emotional states, finding that on platforms like Twitter, they effectively mirror inthe-moment moods. While Derks et al. (2007) highlight thebroader social
dynamics of emoticon use, Kaye and Schweiger (2023) focus on real-time
emotional reflection, though both underscore emojis’ role in compensating for the
emotional limitations of text.

However, these studies predominantly examine Western contexts, leaving a
significant gap in understanding how emojis facilitate emotional expression in
culturally diverse settings like India. Cultural factors play a critical role in shaping
emoji usage, as evidenced by Alzara and Mellor (2024), who identified distinct
emoji preferences in the UAE that reflect local communication norms, and Daud
and McLellan (2016), who analyzed Bruneian Facebook posts to reveal how emojis
intertwine with gender and language to express identity. Although both studies
emphasize cultural specificity, Alzara and Mellor (2024) explore general usage
trends, while Daud and McLellan (2016) delve into demographic details, yet
neither provides a cross-cultural comparison, particularly for multilingual nations
like India where emojis might serve as a unifying tool across linguistic divides.

In the marketing domain, emojis have emerged as powerful tools for enhancing
brand engagement on social media. Ballester et al. (2023) demonstrated that emojis
increase user interaction with eco-friendly restaurants on Instagram, reinforcing
brand identity and trust, while Trivedi and Singh (2021) found that positive emojis
in consumer reviews of Indian food delivery apps correlate with heightened
satisfaction and loyalty.

# METRIC USED FOR THE SENTIMENT ANALYSIS

Here, we employ the VADER (Valence Aware Dictionary and sEntiment
Reasoner), a tool enabling us to analyze emotional aspects of textual
communication, especially in terms of emotion-bearing pictures, called emojis.
They constitute a quicker way of searching the sentiment of micro blog and are not
limited to words but also make use of emojis and other expressions. Consequently,
it is particularly useful in analyzing texts of modern ways of interaction. In the
subsequent sections, we shall explain how the VADER works including its
calculations, and whether it is appropriate in the analysis of such types of texts full
of emojis.

Overview of the VADER Model
It is the combination of sentiment lexicon and emotion-sensing heuristics that
distinguishes VADER's sentiment analysis. It features a precompiled list of words
annotated for sentiment and each word’s value ranges from -4 to 4. One of the main
differences between VADER and standard approaches incorporating various
dictionaries is that VADER uses such features of social networks as emoticons and
emojis, punctuation symbols and capital letters. In this case, VADER is best suited
for the examination of non-formal and condensed chunks of texts where such non-
verbal signs are prevalent.

Key Components of Sentiment Analysis
VADER calculates sentiment scores based on several key components that capture
the different shades of sentiment expressed in a given text. The positive sentiment
score reflects the proportion of the text that conveys a positive emotional tone,
calculated by summing the positive values assigned to individual words or emojis
from the lexicon and normalizing this sum by the overall length of the text. Words
such as "amazing," "great," and positive emojis like "😊" contribute positively to
this score.

Conversely, the negative sentiment score indicates the degree of negativity present
in the text. This score is calculated similarly to the positive score, summing words
or emojis with negative values—words like "terrible," "bad," and negative emojis
like "😢." Additionally, the neutral sentiment score captures the portion of the text

Heuristics and Rule-Based Enhancements
One of the most interesting things about VADER is how it employs heuristics in
order to modify sentiment scores across texts according to language structure and
usage. Rules like those mentioned above take care of tonal differences that may be
lost by unnatural usually lexicon dependent abilities. Thus, VADER based on the

the text the more passionate emotion involved. For instance, “great!!!” would
produce a much greater positive sentiment than just “great,” whereas “terrible!!!"
would be a much stronger negative sentiment than, “terrible.”

Capitalization also plays a role; capitalized words are assigned greater weight in
the sentiment score as they often indicate emphasis. Thus, "AMAZING" would
score higher positively than "amazing." Additionally, degree modifiers (or
intensifiers) suchas "very," "extremely," or "kind of" adjust the polarity of words.
For instance, "very happy" scores higher on the positive scale than just "happy,"
while "kind of sad" lowers the negative score compared to "sad." VADER
effectively handles negation by reversing the polarity of sentiment; for instance,
"not happy" would negate the positive score associated with "happy," ultimately
decreasing the overall sentiment.

Handling Contextual Sentiment
One of the challenges in sentiment analysis, particularly with emojis, is capturing
sentiment that extends beyond the literal meanings of words. VADER is adept at
interpreting the sentiment of short, context-specific texts typical of social media
platforms, making it especially suitable for analyzing emoji usage. For instance, in
the sentence "I'm not sure 😐," the neutral emoji "😐" complements the
uncertainty expressed, resulting in an overall neutral sentiment. Whereas, "I love
it! 😊" would yield a strong positive score due to the combined effects of both the
positive words and the positive emoji.

Advantages of VADER for Emoji Sentiment Analysis
Specifically within this paper’s scope, VADER offers various important
advantages over other approaches when performing sentiment analysis with
symbols such as emojis. Its lexicon is designed for the spoken rather than written
language because of the peculiarities of informal text communication. This
tailoring enhances the effectiveness of VADER in social media outlets which are
more embedded with emojis. More so, English has other gibberish languages that
VADER understands and this provides maximum sentiment information from the
text, which is very useful in trying to understand the usage of emojis specifically
in India.

VADER is also designed to perform well in short text parsing, which is the case
for social media such as X, WhatsApp and Instagram which incorporates frequent
use of emojis. Accuracy thus enables classification of the sentiments through the
analysis of forms of language usage that is informal and often, incomplete. In
addition, VADER also allows simple interpretation of the output that is positive,
negative, neutral and compound for all information provided in text form hence
perception on sentiment from different texts becomes easier.

Limitations and Considerations
Strengths notwithstanding, VADER has its own drawbacks as well. Although
effective in informal communication, it may face challenges in informal contexts
aswell. More vague communicative forms, including sarcasm and irony, and
literature, for example, may also prove to be difficult as they employ culture-based
concepts, for example, emoticons. Whereas it can process negations and degree
modifiers appropriately, it seems that VADER may not appreciate the sentiment in
too stylized or sophisticated language.

Data Collection
The data for this research study was very carefully extracted in the month of
September 2024 with the aid of a web scraping application that was developed
using Python's BeautifulSoup package. It was considered so as to avoid any
ambiguity, while extracting data regarding emoji usage in this case among tweets.
Even though tools such as the twitteR package (Gentry, 2015) have been developed
for the purpose of collecting tweets, the flexibility from using a custom scraper
ensured that the data collected was of optimal quality specific for the objectives of
this research.

In addition to the technical aspects of our data collection, the research was
conducted with consideration of Twitter’s (now X’s) policies and ethical
guidelines. Our data scraping process aimed to utilize publicly available
information while being mindful of Twitter’s Developer Agreement, rate limits,
and user privacy standards. Efforts were made to limit the collection of personally
identifiable information to what is openly accessible, and procedures were
conducted with institutional ethical standards in mind. Our research process took
into account various ethical and policy considerations, contributing to the overall
integrity and reproducibility of our findings.

The research was done on 19 major B2C companies which operate within nine
